WHO Definition HIGH YIELD
📌 Definition "Comprising all the organizations, institutions, and resources devoted to producing health activities." — WHO
Essential shortcut: Any activity that promotes and maintains health
3 Fundamental Objectives 1. Improving the health of the population they serve
2. Responding to people's expectations
3. Providing financial protection against the costs of ill-health

Challenges That Override Rational Policymaking
⚠️ Forces that divert health systems from PHC values 1. Overemphasis on specialist and curative care (hospital-centrism)
2. Commercialization of healthcare delivery
3. Poorly regulated systems
4. Fragmentation in program and institutional design

Note: Strong community-based primary care programs are NOT a challenge — they strengthen rational policymaking.

Universal Health Care (RA 11223)

The UHC Act · Chapters · FAQs · Penal Provisions

⭐ Core Facts MUST KNOW
RA 11223 — UHC Act Signed: February 20, 2019 (Duterte)
Goal: Equitable access to quality, affordable health care + financial protection
WHO Definition of UHC Everyone can afford medical help and have access to appropriate services at an affordable cost
UHC Targets (Western Pacific) 1. OOP ≤ 30–40% of total health expenditure
2. THE ≥ 4–5% of GDP
3. >90% population with prepayment coverage
4. Safety nets for vulnerable sectors
Service entitlement Preventive, promotive, curative, rehabilitative, palliative, dental, mental, emergency

HTA vs HIA — Don't Confuse!
HTA — Health Technology Assessment • Systematic evaluation of health interventions
• Recommends to DOH & PhilHealth what to fund/cover
• Criteria: safety, effectiveness, cost-effectiveness, financial impact, equity
• HTAC = 9 voting members under DOH (later DOST)
• Binding if positive recommendation
HIA — Health Impact Assessment • Evaluates policies, programs, and projects for potential health impacts
• Mandatory under Sec. 33
• Different from HTA — focuses on broader sectoral effects
Population-Based vs Individual-Based Services
Population-Based Services • Health promotion, disease surveillance, vector control
• Recipient = population groups
• Financed by National Government, delivered FREE
• Contracted by DOH
Individual-Based Services • Can be traced to one recipient; accessed in health facility
• Financed via prepayment (PhilHealth, HMOs, private insurance)
• Contracted by PhilHealth
Key Governance Details — Board of Directors
PhilHealth Board — Max 13 members 5 Executive secretaries (Health, DSWD, Budget, Finance, Labor)
3 Expert panel members (public health, management, finance, health economics)
5 Sectoral panel members (direct contributors, indirect contributors, employers, health care providers, elected LCEs)
At least 1 expert + 2 sectoral = women
CEO: Appointed by President of PH, recommended by Board; min 7 years experience
IRR Deadline: 180 days from effectivity · Oversight: Joint Congressional Committee (5 Senate + 5 House) · New PhilHealth Board: within 30 days
Specialist as Primary Care Provider?
A specialist can become a primary care provider BUT must first receive DOH certification in primary care. Not automatic. After specialist treatment, patient must be referred BACK to PCP.

"The application of planning, organizing, staffing, directing, and controlling functions in the most efficient (and effective) manner possible to accomplish meaningful organizational objectives." — Ivancevich & Duening, 2007
Efficiency Completion of task at minimal cost
Effectiveness Completion of task within specific timeline

Health Program Planning

Stages · SWOT · SMART · Disease Prevention Models

⭐ 8 Stages of the Planning Process ALGORITHM
1
Situational Analysis — Collect community health data; geographic distribution; existing programs; organizational & demographic assessment; SWOT
2
Problem Identification & Prioritization — Perceived gap between IS and SHOULD BE; assess magnitude, severity, feasibility, community/government concern; gather root cause data
3
Setting Objectives — SMART objectives; from general to specific; reference point for evaluation
4
Strategy Formulation — List all strategies → select most feasible; consider budget, technology, community participation; Health Communication / Health Education / PSE Change
5
Identify & Sequence Activities — List all activities in correct order; identify prerequisites & simultaneous activities; provide completion checklist
6
Identify Resources — Staff, accommodation, power, equipment, materials, time, skill, information; estimate costs
7
Prepare Action Plans & Schedules — Problem, Objective, Strategy/Activities, Resources, Organization, Control; Gantt chart
8
Monitoring — Are outputs achieved on time? Resources efficiently used? Collaboration with other services?
SWOT Analysis
DomainKey FactorsInternal/External
StrengthsAdvantages, Capabilities, ResourcesInternal · Helpful
WeaknessesDisadvantages, Vulnerabilities, LimitationsInternal · Harmful
OpportunitiesChances, Developments, BenefitsExternal · Helpful
ThreatsObstacles, Politics, Economics, RisksExternal · Harmful

LetterCriterionDescription

⭐ Disease Prevention Theories & Models
ModelCore ConceptKey Elements
Ecological Model Multiple levels of influence; interaction between individual & environment Intrapersonal → Interpersonal → Institutional → Community → Public Policy
Health Belief Model Individual beliefs predict health behavior Perceived Susceptibility · Perceived Severity · Perceived Benefits · Perceived Barriers · Cues to Action · Self-Efficacy
Transtheoretical Model (Stages of Change) Readiness to change behavior Pre-contemplation → Contemplation → Preparation → Action → Maintenance → Termination (can relapse)
