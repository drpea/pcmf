Research Management
How to plan, organize, implement, and evaluate a research project
Definition & Scope

Applying management principles to research units, projects, and programs — from conception to publication.

Covers: concept → protocol → budget → funding → team → data collection → reports → publication/presentation
4 Principles of Management by Objectives
PrincipleKey Idea
EffectivenessDegree to which objectives are achieved; requires clear objectives
EfficiencyGetting things done through people; convergence of work activities
EconomyBest use of available resources at reasonable cost
Decision-MakingShortest decision path; Management by Exception = filter only essential info, prioritize decisions
5 Principles of Organizing
PrincipleDefinition
DepartmentalizationGroup interrelated jobs into units. Functional = by office function; Process = by technical work steps (pre-test → interview → audit → analysis → writing)
Division of WorkAssign tasks to those with training or natural aptitude
Delegation of AuthorityNecessary for growth; gives managerial depth; develops subordinates
Span & ControlLimit number of direct reports per supervisor to what can be effectively directed
Unity of CommandEach person reports to only ONE supervisor (chain of command)
Functional vs Process Departmentalization: Functional = grouped by office type (e.g., medical, nursing, hospitality). Process = grouped by workflow steps (spinning → weaving → dyeing → packing).
3 Main Functions of Management

📌 Planning

Answer questions before they arise. "Planning the actual implementation" — different from the protocol itself.

⚙️ Implementation

On-the-ground execution. Uses work schedules, job descriptions, org charts, training manuals.

📈 Evaluation

Ongoing throughout study. Must be: Timely, Simple, Minimal, Flexible.

4 Steps of Planning (Algorithm)
Look at the situation — understand community, prioritize problems, study available resources (equipment, drugs, time, people)
Set objectives — prepare operational, time-bound targets for each activity
Review obstacles & limitations — People / Equipment / Information / Time / Budget (limitations); Geographic / Climatic / Technical / Social (obstacles) → REMOVE, MODIFY, or NEITHER
Schedule activities — targets, timelines, detailed schedule + budget
For each phase, check 3 areas: Objectives · Activities · Resources

Causality
Correlation ≠ Causality — Understanding the relationship between variables
Correlation vs. Causality HY
Correlation
Mutual relationship (upward or downward) between two variables. Ranges from -1 to +1. Does NOT mean one causes the other.
Causality
Relationship with a valid real-world explanation. Turns correlation's possibility into actuality. Adds mechanism and context.
Classic Example: Ice cream sales ↑ and sunburns ↑ are correlated — but ice cream doesn't cause sunburns. Hot weather causes both. Hot weather → ice cream sales; Hot weather → sunburns.

Ladder of Causality HY
LevelActivityKey QuestionExample
Association (lowest)Observing, correlatingHow do trends in X influence trends in Y?Which symptoms & diseases occur together?
Interventional (middle)Intervening, experimentingHow does Y change if we force a change in X?Reducing smoking → ↓ lung cancer risk
Counterfactual (highest)Imagining, retroactive causal inferenceWas it X that made the difference in Y?If person never smoked → never developed lung cancer
5 Criteria for Medical Causality
Mnemonic: STDR P — Strong association · Temporality · Dose-response · Repeatability · Plausible mechanism
Bradford-Hill Criteria HY
CriterionDescriptionSmoking & Lung Cancer Example
Plausibility Reasonable biological pathway linking exposure to outcome (mechanism needed) 20-year lag time; smoking → carcinogens → cellular mutation → cancer
Consistency Same results in different times, places, and populations ⭐ Smokers have higher lung cancer rates in Western AND Asian populations
Temporality ⭐ CRUCIAL Exposure MUST precede outcome — cause before effect Individuals started smoking BEFORE they developed lung cancer
Strength Magnitude of association — high RR or OR supports causality Smokers have MUCH higher RR of lung cancer vs non-smokers
Specificity "One-to-one" relationship; causal factor specific to outcome; high attributable risk. Lack of specificity does NOT rule out causality. Specific chemicals in cigarettes cause specific cancers
Change in Risk Factor Removing/reducing exposure → decrease in outcome incidence Stopping smoking → significantly lower lung cancer risk
Lack of specificity does NOT rule out causality (many exposures → multiple outcomes)
Clinical Trials & Confounders

GOLD STANDARD RCTs — establish cause & effect in medicine

Problems with RCTs: Unethical (placebo group), Expensive

Observational studies (cohort, case-control, case series) = proxy for RCTs

Confounder = variable that influences BOTH the cause and effect

E.g.: Age confounds exercise–heart disease relationship. Older → exercise less AND higher CVD risk → incorrect conclusion if age is ignored.

Hierarchy of Evidence (highest → lowest): Systematic Reviews → RCTs → Cohort Studies → Case-Control → Case Series/Reports → Expert Opinion

Challenges in Establishing Causality — C·R·C
Confounding
Third variable distorts apparent relationship
Reverse Causality
Unclear if X→Y or Y→X
Confirmation Bias
Looking for info that supports existing beliefs
Statistical Techniques for Causal Inference
TechniquePurpose
Regression AnalysisModel relationship between dependent & independent variables; must account for confounders
Propensity Score MatchingCreates comparable control group in observational studies; reduces confounding bias
Machine Learning ApproachesEstimates heterogeneous treatment effects (how effects vary across individuals)

IMRAD format for original research papers

TypeWord LimitKey Feature

IMRAD Format Structure
TITLE — write LAST; most important part; first contact
↓
ABSTRACT — IMRAC (no Discussion); <150 words unstructured / <250 structured; 3–10 keywords
↓
INTRODUCTION — 3 paragraphs; write after Methods/Results/Discussion
↓
METHODS — past tense; allow replication; check reporting guidelines
↓
RESULTS — past tense; data only (no interpretation); same order as Methods
↓
DISCUSSION — explain meaning; relate to literature; state limitations
↓
CONCLUSION — strong, clear; ideally 1 paragraph; within study scope
↓
REFERENCES + ACKNOWLEDGEMENTS
Total pages: 12–20. Writing order need NOT follow IMRAD — many start with Methods/Results.
Section-by-Section Rules HY
SectionKey RulesCommon Pitfalls
Title Brief, specific, jargon-free; write LAST; must stand alone; aids indexing Using unexplained abbreviations; writing too early
Abstract IMRAC (no Discussion); self-contained; no references; no figures/tables; past tense & passive voice; spell out abbreviations Including references; adding a Discussion section
Introduction 3 paragraphs: ① Background/context → ② Importance/what's known → ③ Rationale/hypothesis/objective Presenting results in intro; being too long
Methods Past tense (change from protocol's future tense); allow replication; include study design, sample, data collection, analysis, ethics Copy-pasting from protocol without changing tense
Results Past tense; data ONLY — no interpretation; all Methods variables appear in same order; both positive AND negative findings Adding interpretation ("this suggests..."); omitting negative results
Discussion Explain meaning of results; relate to similar studies; state limitations; make suggestions for future research Overreaching conclusions; ignoring limitations; repeating results verbatim
Conclusion 1 paragraph; strong & clear; limited by study scope; negative findings = important too Unqualified statements; topics not studied; economic claims without economic data
References Use "References" (not Bibliography); 20–35 refs; consistent citation style Bibliography terminology; inconsistent formatting
Acknowledgements For contributors who DON'T qualify as authors; technical/financial support; general departmental support Including advisers (they are co-authors, not acknowledgements)
Tables vs. Figures in Results
Use TABLE when:
Exact values are important (mean ± SD, range, comparison of specific numbers). Label goes ABOVE the table.
Use GRAPH/FIGURE when:
Trends and relationships matter more than exact values. Label goes BELOW the figure.
Don't use tables/figures if data can be stated in ONE sentence. Always cite in text BEFORE they appear. Number correctly.

  • Spell out numbers below ten; never start sentence with numeral
  • Always cite sources for non-original statements; punctuation comes BEFORE superscript

MethodBest ForKey Notes

10 Principles for Designing Tables HY
  1. Tables should be numbered (Table 1, Table 2…); Table 1 = Baseline Characteristics
  2. Title: brief, self-explanatory, placed ON TOP
  3. Headnote: clarification below title (in parentheses)
  4. Headings: clear, concise, capitalized; no generic labels
  5. Data: ordered by size, chronology, alphabet, or geography
  6. Comparisons: place close to each other
  7. No table too large (don't reduce font to force-fit)
  8. Vertical arrangement preferred over horizontal
  9. Footnotes at bottom: explanatory notes, sources, abbreviations, units, pattern of analysis
  10. Simple and self-explanatory; include normal ranges when using values
Types of Tables
TypeDescriptionExample
SimpleOne classification in rows and/or columns; final data presentationHeight frequency table
MasterSeveral classifications in rows AND columns; large datasetsPeriodic Table
Special – Frequency DistributionQuantitative data split into class intervals with frequenciesAge groups vs. count
Special – Four-fold (2×2)Most common in observational studies; computes OR and RRCase-control exposure table
Special – CorrelationTwo-way frequency distribution; shows correlation between variablesExposure vs. outcome cross-table
Frequency Table Construction Rules
  • Class interval size: Not too large or small; uniform across table
  • Number of classes: Ideally 8–15 rows (too many → empty cells; too few → loss of detail)
  • Headings: Proper and clear
  • Documentation: Source and pattern of analysis in footnote
Types of Graphs vs. Diagrams HY

GRAPHS

GraphUse When…
Pie Chart ⭐Nominal data; small # of categories (≤2 distributions); proportions of a whole
Simple BarCategorical data; one variable; vertical or horizontal
Multiple BarRelated sets of data; grouped bars; each bar differently colored
Component/Subdivided BarStacked bar; shows parts of a whole; >2 distributions (preferred over pie)

DIAGRAMS

DiagramUse When…
Frequency CurveSmooth hand-drawn histogram; compare sets; cumulative frequency (Ogives)
Scatter DiagramRelationship between 2 variables; dots clustering → linear relationship
Line DiagramChanges over TIME; arithmetic scale = absolute change; logarithmic = relative change
PictogramNon-technical audience; uses small pictures/symbols
Statistical MapGeographic/administrative data; shading intensity = magnitude
5 Principles for Diagram Construction
  1. Completely self-explanatory (title, source, scale, legend)
  2. KEY Title placed BELOW the graph/diagram
  3. Frequency on vertical axis; classification on horizontal
  4. Frequency scale starts at zero (if not possible, show a break)
  5. Equal increments on arithmetic scale = equal numerical units
Dummy Tables
  • Help outline how trial results will be reported
  • Created BEFORE data collection tools are finalized; appended to protocol
  • Enables advance planning; keeps team aligned with objectives
  • Interim and final analysis should adhere to dummy table structure
  • Should follow CONSORT statement structure: flow diagram + baseline table + outcomes table + adverse events table
Research Paper Reporting
Oral presentations, poster presentations, and online conferences
What a Research Talk IS and IS NOT
IS:
✅ Give the audience an intuitive feel for your idea
✅ Make them eager to read your paper
IS NOT:
❌ To impress with brainpower
❌ To tell them ALL you know
❌ To present all technical details
Your presentation should be NARROW and DEEP — focused on the KEY IDEA, not wide and shallow.
Initial Planning Checklist
  • Type of talk: Summary only vs. full discussion? Theme of seminar?
  • Time allotted: Do NOT go over the time limit (impolite to organizer and next presenter)
  • Expectations for content: What does the audience want to know?
  • Composition of audience: Experts (skip basic methods) vs. general (explain more)

Content split: 20% Motivation + 80% Key Idea

Presentation Preparation Steps
Start preparing early
Identify important issues to discuss more deeply
Outline your talk
List important points and prioritize (most → least important)
Rehearse 10 times — edit and shorten each time
Slide Design — BSCC Rules HY
PrincipleSpecific Rules
Make it BIG Readable from 6 ft away from screen (no minimum font size — use 6 ft rule)
Keep it Simple ≤6 lines of text per slide · ≤7 words per line · Avoid text-only slides in a row · Use graphs/images in between · GRAPH > TABLE ⭐
Make it Clear Sans serif fonts (Helvetica, Arial, Calibri) · No italics · Font >24 pt (minimum 18) · No ALL CAPS sentences · Contrasting colors (avoid red-on-blue, yellow-on-white)
Be Consistent Same color + visual concept throughout · No highly creative designs (formal presentation) · Audience should get the point within 5 seconds
DO NOT read your slides! "Otherwise, just give them the PowerPoint." — reading slides disengages the audience.
Presentation Day Tips

Before presenting:

  • Check room, equipment, software compatibility ⭐
  • Send talk by email as backup
  • Rate: 100 words/min; 1–3 min/slide; 30 sec minimum per slide

While presenting:

  • Keep eye contact; identify nodders
  • Stand to the SIDE of screen; no pacing
  • Laser pointer: flash on/off, not back-and-forth
  • Be ENTHUSIASTIC
Q&A Tips ⭐
Questions = golden opportunity to connect!
  • Always repeat each question; show appreciation
  • If question clarifies ambiguity → answer immediately; if covered later → inform them
  • Ask: "Did I answer your question sufficiently?"
  • Avoid prolonged discussions with one person → "Maybe we can discuss this later"
  • If you can't answer: say so (don't apologize), offer to research, suggest resources, ask audience
  • Have backup slides ready for anticipated questions

Phases of an Epidemic (WHO 2018)
#PhaseDescription
1Introduction/EmergenceDisease emerges in a community
2Localized TransmissionSporadic infections or localized transmission
3AmplificationHuman-to-human transmission → sustained outbreak (COVID-19 example)
4Reduced TransmissionDue to population immunity or effective interventions
Why Do Epidemics Occur?

1. New Disease

  • Introduction of a new disease (e.g., polio brought by settlers)
  • Recognition of existing disease by another name (e.g., dengue ← hemorrhagic fever)
  • Zoonotic transmission (e.g., COVID-19)

2. Flare-up of Old Disease (changes in E-A-H)

  • Environment: calamities → leptospirosis, diarrhea
  • Agent: ↑ virulence, new strain, mutation (e.g., COVID variants)
  • Host: ↓ immunity, ↓ vaccination (e.g., Dengvaxia → measles 2019)
Epidemic Patterns & Curves HY
PatternDescriptionCurve ShapeExample
Point Source All exposed to SAME source during a BRIEF period; cases occur within ONE incubation period Steep upslope, gradual downslope (sharp peak) Food poisoning after a party
Continuous Source Same source over MORE than a brief period Flatter and wider peak Cholera from contaminated well (Broad St. pump, John Snow)
Propagated/Progressive Person-to-person spread; cases over SEVERAL incubation periods Multiple peaks consistent with incubation period Measles, malaria (vectors), HIV (vehicles)
Mixed Features of BOTH common-source AND propagated Combined characteristics Initial contamination → subsequent person-to-person spread
Uses of epidemic curves:
  1. Identify type of exposure from shape
  2. Estimate incubation period (interval between exposure and peak)
  3. Suggest possible etiologic agents (if time of exposure known)
  4. Determine time of exposure (if agent/incubation period known)

StrategyMethodsExamples

6 Reasons for Epidemic Investigation
  1. Stop the current outbreak from spreading
  2. Provide scientific explanation (describe new diseases; learn about known ones)
  3. Evaluate existing preventive measures
  4. React to and calm public/political concerns (prevent speculation, panic, fake news)
  5. Prevent future similar outbreaks
  6. Train epidemiologists
History: John Snow & Field Epidemiology
  • London, 1854 — severe cholera epidemic in Soho
  • John Snow (anesthesiologist) = Father of Field Epidemiology
  • Method: rigorous data gathering → spot map to plot cases → located water pumps
  • Found cases clustered around Broad Street pump → pump handle removed → cholera declined
  • These methods are used in epidemic investigations today
10 Steps of Epidemic Investigation HY
Identify investigation team and resources — read about disease; list equipment; invite experts
Establish existence of outbreak — are cases above baseline? Check census/surveillance/registries; rule out increased awareness or diagnostic changes
Verify diagnosis — check manifestations + diagnostic tests; perform lab tests if needed
Establish case definition & search for cases — case definition includes: Person (type of illness) + Place (suspected exposure) + Time (based on incubation period). Use passive (hospitals) AND active (contact tracing) surveillance
Find cases systematically + descriptive epidemiologic study — epidemic curve (time) + spot map (place) → clusters/patterns
Develop hypothesis — "Exposure X is associated with Y." Includes source of exposure + means of transmission. Generated from: risk factors, spot map, epidemic curve
Evaluate hypothesis — analytic studies: Cohort → RR; Case-control → OR. Study environment, sanitary conditions, get samples
Implement control and preventive measures
Communicate findings — oral AND written reports to local health authorities
Maintain surveillance — check for new cases. Outbreak declared OVER: 2 incubation periods with no new cases
Steps can be done simultaneously or in different order based on circumstances!

SGD Case Algorithm: Botulism Outbreak (Buenos Aires, 1998)
2 cases of botulism reported (Jan 13) → same bus company, same route
↓ Step 1-3: Verify dx, notify stakeholders
Press release → 7 more cases identified. All males, morning shift, ate at terminal home
↓ Step 4-5: Case definition + Descriptive epi
Epidemic curve = Point Source (single exposure). Earliest onset Jan 5 → exposure Jan 3-4 (18-36h incubation)
↓ Step 6: Hypothesis
H: Morning shift drivers → ate/drank at terminal home Jan 3-7 → botulism
↓ Step 7: Retrospective Cohort Study → compute RR
Matambre: RR = 8.1* (p=0.0002) · Sauce: RR = 4.6 (p=0.009) ✅ Significant
↓ Steps 8-10
Control measures → Investigate matambre producer → Communicate findings → Surveillance (2 incubation periods)
RR Calculation note: When 0 exposed persons got ill (or 0 in denominator) → adjust by changing 0 to 1 to get a numerical result (acceptable statistical approach)
