Intro to Family Medicine & Family Assessment Tools

Biopsychosocial (BPS) Model MUST KNOW
ComponentWhat it addressesExample
BiologicalPhysical disease, pathology, lab findingsHeart disease, infection
PsychologicalEmotions, cognition, mental health, phobiasFear → fatal cardiac event
SocialFamily, economics, culture, relationshipsInheritance motive; poverty
🚨 Key Contrast
Biomedical: Treats body as machine — find/fix broken part only.
BPS: Considers all three factors as interconnected — treats the whole person.
  • Introduced by George Engel, 1977
  • Semiotics — symptoms are interpreted signs, not just facts
  • Constructivism — illness shaped by experience and social context
  • Individual Reality — past beliefs/experiences shape current illness response
  • Modern research: hopelessness → ↑ ischemic heart disease risk; caregivers → ↑ procoagulant load
PFC Matrix MUST KNOW

Patient-centered, Family-focused, Community-oriented. Operationalizes BPS in primary care.

ComponentFocusTools / MethodsKey concept
Patient-centered Patient perception; biomedical & psychosocial data CEA Method, SOAP note Address health misconceptions through education; tailor-fit management
Family-focused Family as unit of care; shared resources, genetics, behaviors Genogram, Family APGAR, Lifeline, Family Map Family = greatest ally; identify breadwinner, caregiver, decision-maker
Community-oriented Links patient/family to public health & social resources Ecomap, SCREEM/SCREEM-RES Uses SDH building blocks; referral, collaboration, networking
⚙ CEA Method (Patient Engagement Algorithm) Order Matters!
1
Catharsis — Let patient express feelings FIRST. Do not skip this. Cannot proceed to action without catharsis.
2
Education — Correct misconceptions; explain the illness in understandable terms.
3
Action — Co-create management plan with patient. Done last.
Exam Trap
You CANNOT proceed to Action without completing Catharsis first. Answer = C → E → A always.

⚙ 5 Steps to Evaluate Quality of Health Indicators
1
Integrity — Is data complete, valid, and representative of target population?
2
Personal attributes — Is it consistent by age, sex, population at risk?
3
Place attributes — Expected spatial patterns? Rural vs. urban distribution?
4
Temporal attributes — Seasonal or cyclic patterns? Historical trends?
5
Plausibility — Does it make sense compared to other data sources? (e.g., Low MMR despite poor prenatal care → questionable)
Decision Matrix — Priority Setting Algorithm ALGORITHM
Criteria & Weights
  • Magnitude — How many affected (30%)
  • Feasibility/Sustainability of health program (30%)
  • Impact — Long-term disability/death (20%)
  • Community concern — Perception of seriousness (20%)
How to calculate:
1
Score each problem 1–4 per criterion
2
Score × Weight = Criterion score
3
Sum all criterion scores = Total
4
Highest total = Priority #1
5
Next: Problem Tree → Objective Tree

Problem Tree: Causes → Problem → Effects. Objective Tree: Activities → General Objective → Effects of Intervention.

Primary Health Care & Primary Care

Alma Ata · E.L.E.M.E.N.T.S · Levels of Participation · UHC

Alma Ata Declaration (1978) FOUNDATIONAL
  • First international declaration underlining importance of PHC
  • Declared in Almaty, Kazakhstan, September 6–12, 1978
  • Urgent call to protect health of all by Year 2000
  • Goal: "Health for All"
PHC Definition (Alma Ata)
Essential health care based on practical, scientifically sound, and socially acceptable methods & technology made universally accessible at a cost the community and country can afford, in the spirit of self-reliance and self-determination.
PHC Objectives (Alma Ata)
  • Health for all
  • Health is a Fundamental Human Right
  • Equity
  • Appropriate Technology
  • Inter-sectoral Development
  • Community Participation

Rationale for PHC PHILIPPINES CONTEXT
  • Too much concentration of services in urban areas
  • Brain drain — professionals leave for better pay abroad
  • Overdependence on imported health technologies
  • Domination by foreign multinational pharmaceutical companies
  • Poor health financing (→ solved by UHC Act)
  • Top-down decision-making in health
  • Priorities focused on curative care
  • Colonial mentality
  • Western-oriented medical education
  • Poor motivation of government health personnel

Community Health Workers Training

CHW Definition · Selection · Course Planning · Supervision

CHW — Key Definitions & Roles MUST KNOW
APHA 3-Part Definition
  1. Trusted, knowledgeable frontline health personnel from the communities they serve
  2. Serves as liaison/link/intermediary between health/social services and the community
  3. Bridges the gap — links health care system and social services to the people
Three Main Roles
  • Service Providers — screenings, IMCI, prenatal/postnatal care, records
  • Health Educators — culturally appropriate education in community settings
  • Community Organizers — identify needs, build capacity, facilitate support groups
End Goal of CHW
Provide access to quality, culturally competent health care delivery where doctors, nurses, and midwives are absent or scarce.
CHW Selection Criteria KNOW FOR MCQ
✅ Selection Criteria
  • Ideally selected by the community (improves trust & acceptance)
  • Well-respected; 8–10 years of basic education (up to high school)
  • Mature, responsible, capable of sound decisions
  • Genuine interest — not motivated by power/money/influence
  • NOT forced or politically appointed
  • Family support is crucial for sustainability
  • Physically and mentally healthy
❌ NOT Used as Criteria (WHO)
🚨 WHO Rec
Age and marital status should NOT be used as criteria.
Workload Standard
Part-time CHW: ≤25 families (ideal) | Maximum: 50 families
🧠 Qualities Mnemonic — 3C's, 2V's, 1 P.T.
3C's: Compassionate · Committed · Creative
2V's: Voluntary spirit · Visionary leadership
1 P.T.: Patient · Team-worker

Triad of Causation — Variables in Disease Emergence ALGORITHM

🦠 AGENT

  • Genetic instability of microorganisms
  • Genetic plasticity → adapt to new ecological niches
  • Mutations → antibiotic resistance (MRSA, drug-resistant TB)
  • High mutation rates: Influenza, COVID-19
  • Most EID are historically zoonotic

👤 HOST

  • Susceptibility to infection
  • Demographics & behavior
  • Human movement (key driver!)
  • Crowding, population growth
  • Risky behaviors (IV drug use, unprotected sex)
  • Portals of entry: skin, respiratory, GI

🌍 ENVIRONMENT

  • Climate change → El Niño/La Niña → ↑ vector-borne
  • Deforestation → Zika, Dengue
  • Water storage → Aedes aegypti emergence
  • Rubber tires → Aedes albopictus breeding
  • War & natural disasters
  • Agricultural development
High-Yield: Environment-Disease Links
Aedes aegypti emerged ~5000 yrs ago in N. Africa from human water storage practices → yellow fever, dengue, chikungunya.
Aedes albopictus breeds in rubber tires; same diseases.
Cholera 1832: Spread along travel routes; slowed in winter, surged in summer → HOST (travel behavior) factor.

International Health Regulations (IHR 2005) MUST KNOW
What is IHR?
Legally binding international instrument on disease prevention, surveillance, and response. Adopted by all WHO member countries. Formal declaration = PHEIC.
Goals of IHR
  • Improve notification procedures
  • Identify PHEICs
  • Facilitate global communication during emergencies
  • Mitigate consequences through coordinated response
4 Criteria to Determine Notifiability
  • Is the public health impact serious?
  • Is the event unusual or unexpected?
  • Is there significant risk of international spread?
  • Is there significant risk of international travel/trade restrictions?

Notification must occur within 24 hours of assessment.

MCQ

Practice Questions

Click an option to reveal the answer and explanation

1. A patient with hypertension mentions she stopped taking her medication because her husband left and she has no one to remind her. Using the BPS model, which factor is primarily driving her non-compliance?
A Biological — her disease is worsening
B Psychological — she has no motivation
C Social — loss of social support from spouse
D Pharmacological — the drug is too expensive
✅ Social factors (family support, social networks) are a key BPS component. The spouse served as a social support mechanism, and its loss is the primary driver here.
2. In the CEA method, a physician immediately explains the disease mechanism to the patient after history-taking. What went wrong?
A The physician was too technical
B The physician skipped Catharsis before Education
C The physician should have made an Action plan first
D Nothing; this is appropriate in time-limited consults
✅ CEA order is strict: Catharsis → Education → Action. You CANNOT proceed to Education without first allowing the patient to express their feelings (Catharsis).
3. A medical team conducts house-to-house interviews asking about recent illnesses and vaccination status. What type of data source is this?
A Primary — directly gathered from community members
B Secondary — it uses routine health reports
C Primary — uses already published data
D Secondary — based on past hospital records
✅ Primary data provides direct evidence about an event. House-to-house interviews are direct data collection from the source population.
4. Which is TRUE regarding Primary vs. Secondary data?
A Primary data requires more analytical effort to extract indicators
B Secondary data has greater cost in time and money
C Secondary data has lower cost but more difficulty stratifying indicators
D Primary data is less temporally comparable
✅ Secondary data = lower cost + more timely BUT more analytical effort required + more difficulty stratifying. Primary data = greater cost in time/money BUT greater control over quality.
5. An epidemiologist notes a community's official report shows very low maternal mortality, but a separate survey reveals extremely poor prenatal care rates. Which step in quality evaluation of health indicators applies?
A Examine consistency with temporal attributes
B Examine consistency with personal attributes
C Examine plausibility in relation to other data sources
D Examine consistency with place attributes
✅ When you question an indicator based on discrepancy with other data sources, that's Step 5: Examine Plausibility. Low MMR despite poor prenatal care = implausible without investigation.
6. A barangay in a far-flung area has no clinic. Community members identify a trusted local, train her in prenatal care, and she monitors pregnant women. Which level of PHC participation is this?
A Hospital/Clinic-Based
B Community-Oriented
C Community-Based
D Community-Managed
✅ Community-Based: community actively plans activities together with health professionals/CHW; shared decision-making; CHW + NGO involvement. Community-Managed would require no external players at all.
7. Which community organizing principle states that leaders must be accountable to the people at all times and emerge through concrete action?
A Consciousness-raising
B Self-reliance
C Participatory and Mass-based
D Democratic leadership
✅ Democratic leadership = group-centered, NOT leader-oriented. Leaders emerge through concrete action, NOT externally appointed. Leaders are accountable to the people at ALL times.
8. A CHW candidate is described as a respected community member, married with children, 35 years old, with 10 years of basic education. According to WHO, which criterion should NOT be used in selecting her?
A Marital status
B Community respect
C Educational attainment
D Physical health status
✅ WHO recommends that AGE and MARITAL STATUS should NOT be used as criteria for CHW selection.
9. The first international NON-governmental agency in international health was:
A World Health Organization
B International Red Cross
C Pan American Sanitary Bureau
D OIHP
✅ International Red Cross = first international NON-governmental agency. Founded 1864, Switzerland. WHO is intergovernmental; PASB was from 1902; OIHP was 1909 (formal governmental).
10. During the 1832 cholera pandemic, cases decreased in winter and surged in summer. The best explanation based on Triad of Causation is:
A Seasonal mutation of the Vibrio cholerae bacterium (Agent)
B Seasonal human travel patterns following trade routes (Host)
C Temperature effects on bacterial survival (Environment)
D Breakdown of public health sanitation in winter
✅ Host factor: cholera followed trade/travel routes. Travel slowed in winter → outbreak slowed. Travel increased in summer → outbreak surged. Human movement behavior = HOST variable.
11. An assessment tool assesses a family's connections to church, neighborhood, and local businesses using directional arrows and line weights. This BEST describes:
A Genogram
B SCREEM
C Ecomap
D Family Lifeline
✅ Ecomap = pictorial representation of family's connections to external systems. Uses arrows (energy flow), line thickness (strength), and jagged lines (stressful connections).
12. Which community organizing model is BEST suited for a community facing high rates of adolescent pregnancy where experts are brought in to set goals and create plans with data?
A Locality Development Model
B Social Planning Model
C Social Action Model
D Community Partnership Model
✅ Social Planning Model = uses experts/task force; addresses routine social problems with factual data; useful for education, child development, environmental health, reproductive health planning.
13. According to DOH Four-Door Approach, which door refers to preventing importation of emerging infectious diseases through airports and seaports?
A Door 1
B Door 2
C Door 3
D Door 4
✅ Door 2 = Point of Entry (airports and seaports). Door 1 = point of origin; Door 3 = hospital/community care; Door 4 = epidemic surge.
14. A family scores 5 on the SCREEM-RES. The CORRECT interpretation is:
A Adequate family resources (13–18)
B Moderately inadequate resources (7–12)
C Severely inadequate resources (0–6)
D Cannot be interpreted without qualitative SCREEM
✅ SCREEM-RES scoring: 13–18 = adequate; 7–12 = moderately inadequate; 0–6 = severely inadequate family resources. Score of 5 = severely inadequate.
15. A trainee immunization course requires mastery of when NOT to give vaccines. What skills does this require?
A Knowledge only
B Manual skills only
C Knowledge and Decision-making
D All of K, A, C, M, D
✅ "When not to give" requires K (knowledge of contraindications) + D (decision-making: applying knowledge to decide). This is consistent with the sample immunization course planning table.
